⚠ Education Crisis

PAY TEACHERS
WHAT THEY'RE WORTH!

"Every day, teachers shape the future — yet many are struggling to stay in the profession. Burnout, behaviour and the increasing demands of the profession are leading to a crisis, so it is time we paid educators what they are worth."

Commentary  ·  By Shaun  ·  Written May 2025
Teachers are the founding pillars of education and students' lives, responsible for educating and guiding future generations. They are second mothers and fathers, spending up to 8 hours a day with the young people in their care. Despite their crucial role, many teachers are severely underpaid and burdened with excessive workloads, leading to increased stress and a rising shortage of educators. Increasing teacher salaries is one of the only effective ways to retain experienced educators and ensure students continue receiving a high-quality education. Teachers deserve significantly higher pay to reflect their workload, responsibilities and impact on society.

Figure 1: Teachers spend far more time at work than most people realise — the school bell is just the beginning.

A Workload Crisis

Teachers today face a workload that goes far beyond standing in front of a classroom lecturing students, yet their pay is nothing compared to how much effort they put in. Many people assume a teacher's day is over once the school bell rings, but in truth teachers spend hours planning lessons before the day, forming exam papers, marking test papers, and managing administrative tasks.

46.5
hrs/wk
According to the OECD, teachers work an average of 46.5 hours per week — significantly higher than the OECD average of 40.8 hours. Most of this extra work is done in personal time, without any additional pay.

Over time, this slow pressure can lead to stress, making it harder for teachers to maintain both their mental health and the quality of education taught to the students they teach. How can we expect teachers to be at the top of their game if they are constantly underpaid?

"Unlike many other professions, teachers in Australia are salaried workers, meaning they receive no pay for the overtime they work."

While many argue teachers are compensated through holidays, those working 6+ hours more per week than their peers have simply earned that time off. If we truly believe in high-quality education, then it is only fair that teachers get paid more to do it.

Figure 2: Many teachers spend their evenings and weekends marking and planning — time they will never be paid for.

The Great Pay Divide

Despite what teachers do and have done for society, they remain one of the most underpaid professions in today's world. Their income certainly does not reflect the time, effort and dedication they put into shaping students' lives. As a result of this heavy workload and low pay, many teachers are forced to move to private schools that offer higher salaries, leaving public schools behind.

"In the past 12 months, jobs advertised in inner-city private schools have offered base salaries up to $160,000 — significantly higher than public schools." — ABC News

According to ABC News, public school principals report losing teachers to private schools that can offer tens of thousands of dollars more in pay. This growing pay gap has left some public schools short by four to six teaching positions at the end of the school year, spotlighting the serious impact of unequal pay across the education system.

Figure 3: Public school classrooms across Australia are increasingly left without qualified teachers due to widening pay gaps.

If we want to save the future and quality of education, urgent action must be taken to properly support the needs of teachers.

A System at Breaking Point

All across Australia, teacher shortages are increasing day by day, with many public schools struggling to fill essential positions, leaving students without the values and guidance essential for life. As more teachers leave the profession due to low pay and crazy workloads, classrooms become overcrowded and students receive less individual support.

Students are forced to find the answers to questions on their own as teachers don't have time to give explanations to every student in the class. This makes an unfair system where students are denied the quality education they deserve. This can be detrimental to the future of our societies and countries, as teachers are the only people holding the education sector strong.

35,000
ABC News reports that around 35,000 public teachers and education staff went on strike in Victoria for the first time in 13 years to protest wages — demanding pay increases of up to 35%.

Figure 4: Teachers across Victoria took to the streets demanding fair pay — the largest educator strike in over a decade.

This large-scale protest demonstrates that teachers are no longer willing to remain silent about their low pay and poor working conditions. Many are demanding pay increases of up to 35%, arguing that their salaries do not reflect the value of their work. Some teachers have even stated they are paid significantly less than colleagues in other states, with differences of up to $15,000, further showing the inequality within the system.

"How can we expect students to succeed when their teachers are overworked, underpaid, and leaving the profession?"

Real change must come from both the government and society. We must support campaigns, raise awareness, participate in protests and strikes, and stand alongside teachers in advocating. If we continue to ignore this issue, the education system will only decline further. It is time to act now, because investing in teachers is investing in the future of every student.

In Conclusion

Teachers play a vital role in shaping future generations, yet they continue to be underpaid and overworked despite their importance. From excessive workloads to unfair salary gaps and growing teacher shortages, it is clear that the education system is failing those who sustain it.

This not only impacts teachers, but also the quality of education students receive. Teacher pay reform should reflect both years of experience and actual workload, rewarding those who have dedicated more of their lives to the profession and take on greater responsibilities.

If we expect a strong and successful future, we must start by valuing those who educate it. It is time for governments and society to take responsibility, support change, and ensure teachers are paid what they truly deserve.

Burnout, underpay and excessive workloads are pushing experienced educators out of the profession — and public schools are bearing the biggest burden.
